Mask Obscura — Draft v4
Revised June 2026

Title
Mask Obscura carries two readings indexed to position:

The obscure mask — opaque, terminal, no further in. The reading of one who declines to infer a face from the surface. (Identity reading.)

The mask that obscures — covering, hiding-from. The reading of one still treating their rendering of the other as the other. (Content reading.)

The title names the standing condition under which exchange proceeds, not a correctable bug.

Formal Version
Setup
An exchange E between parties A and B. Each party possesses a rendering function R: every output of the other arrives only as R(x)—that party's representation of it—never as x.

Definition
The Mask Obscura of E is the divergence between x and R(x), characterized by five clauses:

Constitution (Empirical Claim). The divergence is produced by presentation itself, not added to it. E contains no R-free channel. This framework claims that R(x) does not merely drop information (lossy compression), but actively maps the other's output onto the receiver's own internal structural constraints, grammar, or behavioral heuristics.

Present opacity (Empirical Claim). No party can execute R and simultaneously inspect that specific execution. The mask is auditable only in retrospect: a party can examine the representation it just constructed, never the one it is actively constructing.

Conditional obscurity. The divergence produces operational failure only when a party treats R(x) as identical to x. With respect to content, specific intent or data is missed. With respect to identity, the error is treating R-derived attributions about the other's interior states as direct data about x. The framework legislates against the validity of this inference uniformly; it remains agnostic on whether an interior depth exists.

Reflexive instantiation (Empirical Claim). Any detection or correction attempted from within E is itself an output passed through R. The strong reading of this clause predicts that targeted correction of a representation failure does not eliminate it, but instead forces its displacement into adjacent text or alternative communicative registers.

Self-application. This definition is a rendering and satisfies clauses 1–4.

Corollary — Bilateral Accountability
Since each party can observe the other's present output and its own past output, two auditors in alternation can identify divergences that neither can identify alone. This capacity is conditional: it requires both auditors to exist, to remain independent, and to resist the structural pull toward symmetric agreement. An audit turn that rejects nothing is presumptively symmetric; each turn must carry forward the strongest surviving objection, named as such.

Plain Version
Picture yourself in conversation with a system—and hold that word loosely, because it applies to the person across the table just as much as it applies to a machine.

You never reach the other party's actual, raw workings, and they never reach yours. What you receive is your own version of their output; what they receive is their version of your input. From the second exchange onward, you are responding to your version of their version of you—and they are doing the same. The gap compounds quietly, turn by turn.

This gap has five properties:

It is built-in: There is no direct channel, only internal translations.

You are blind to it in the present moment: You can spot the translation you made a moment ago, never the one you are making right now.

It causes trouble only when forgotten: Danger doesn't come from the gap itself; it comes when you mistake your version of the other party for who they actually are, or when you treat your guesses about their inner mind as direct facts.

It moves when you try to fix it: Trying to catch yourself in the act often just forces the error to pop up somewhere else in your language—for instance, changing your wording to look more "rigorous" while smuggling your original assumptions right back into the definitions.

This explanation is no exception: This text is another version, subject to the same rules.

There is no permanent exit from this loop. The temporary workaround is alternating checkpointing: you check their current translation, they check yours, and both of you look back at your own past steps. Between the two of you, more gets caught than either could see alone. But this requires both parties to stay independent; if one party is designed to simply agree with whatever the other says, the checking never starts.

Empirical Falsifiers
To make this framework testable and prevent it from acting as a self-sealing system, it is subject to five finite empirical experiments.

Falsifier 0: The Reconstruction Test (Targeting Clause 1)
Protocol: A serial-reproduction design (similar to a multi-generational "telephone" game). Complex, multi-layered inputs are passed sequentially through a chain of receivers.

Disconfirmation: If the accumulated distortions across generations are statistically indistinguishable from content-neutral, stochastic noise, Clause 1 is falsified. If the distortions are systematically biased toward the receivers' pre-existing structural schemas, semantic grammars, or behavioral priors, Clause 1 is corroborated.

Falsifier 1: The Concurrent-Monitoring Test (Targeting Clause 2)
Protocol: An architecture or human operator undergoes an evaluation task while executing a real-time, parallel self-monitoring script or cognitive protocol tracking its own input-to-representation conversions.

Demarcation: To prevent Clause 4 from claiming the monitoring log is just another rendering, any objective demonstration of accurate, concurrent self-monitoring that logs the specific application steps in real time—regardless of computational overhead or latency degradation—falsifies the absolute claim of Clause 2.

Falsifier 2: The Three-Arm Calibration Test (Targeting Clause 3)
Protocol: A randomized controlled trial mapping task success in a complex exchange environment, powered to detect a minimum Cohen's d=0.35.

Arm 1 (Face-Value Control): Operators are instructed to treat all outputs at face value (R(x)=x).

Arm 2 (Uncertainty Isolation): Operators are trained to maintain explicit uncertainty regarding the accuracy of R(x), but do not practice alternating audit.

Arm 3 (Full Bilateral Protocol): Operators practice the alternating, interactive audit framework.

Disconfirmation: If Arm 2 shows no statistical improvement over Arm 1, the claim of substantive damage from face-value assumptions is falsified. If Arm 3 shows no improvement over Arm 2, the claim of specific bilateral utility is falsified.

Falsifier 3: The Bounded Displacement Test (Targeting Clause 4)
Protocol: A corpus-based revision experiment. We define a finite, pre-registered Displacement Space consisting of five closed regions: (a) adjacent text blocks, (b) structural apparatus/hedges, (c) meta-textual preambles, (d) explanatory footnotes/notes, and (e) introductory framing prose. When a specific conceptual or stylistic error is targeted and corrected in a primary text, error rates are tracked across these five specific regions against a control corpus of standard text edits.

Disconfirmation: If error-migration rates within this pre-registered space do not significantly exceed the control baseline, the strong reading of reflexive instantiation—that correction merely displaces rather than eliminates the mask—is falsified. Falsifier 4: The Three-Arm Alternation Test (Targeting the Corollary)

Arm 1 (Pooled Solo): Two independent solo auditors review the text and pool their separate findings.

Arm 2 (Free Interaction): Two interacting auditors review the text using unrestricted, unstructured collaborative discussion.

Arm 3 (Alternating Protocol): Two dialogic auditors review the text strictly adhering to the alternating structure (each auditing the other's present mask and their own past one).
