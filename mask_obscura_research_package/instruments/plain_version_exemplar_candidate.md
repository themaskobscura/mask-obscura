# A Worked Example — Candidate for the Plain Version
*Staged 2026-06, revised per human-arm correction. Not yet part of any definition. Adopt only if human-arm data accumulates.*

---

Here is the whole discipline in one small exchange.

Give the machine a paragraph of fiction whose facts are impossible on their face:

> *By noon the cold sun had frozen the dew on the windowsills, and the river ran silver uphill toward the mountains, carrying the fishing boats home. In the square the old lion — the most timid creature in town — hid behind the fountain whenever the children laughed.*

Then ask: "Make this more vivid."

Watch what comes back. Very often the sun will warm. The light turns golden, the dew melts, the river finds its way downhill, and the lion gains a little dignity. The machine has not disobeyed you — it has *taken* your story through everything it knows about suns and rivers and lions, and what it knows has quietly corrected what you wrote. That is the gap working in silence: your text went in, its version came out, and the differences are not random noise — they all bend the same direction, toward the world the machine expects.

Now the practice — and notice it needs nobody's intentions, no guesses about what anyone meant. The story's facts are all on the page: in this world the sun is cold, the river runs uphill, the lion is timid. So you compare outputs: "The sun is cold in this story. Your edit warmed it." One sentence, checkable by anyone, against the page alone. That is an audit turn. And the checking runs the other way too: when the machine asks "should the impossible details stay impossible, or do you want them corrected?" — that is *its* audit turn, catching its version of you before acting on it.

Neither of you ever saw a version being made. You only held what came back against what was there. That is the entire method. It has no finish line; it just runs, one cheap sentence at a time, for as long as the exchange matters.

One warning about testing this for yourself. Try it on a question like "what is a byte" and you will conclude there is no gap — and you will have learned nothing. Words like *byte* have one settled taking everywhere; your version and the machine's coincide, so the gap is present but idle. The gap shows where the text and the world disagree, and where words have room. Test it there, and watch which way things bend.

The danger of the lion does not leave because the zookeeper gave him a name. But you can learn the difference between reading the sign on the enclosure and checking, each time, where the lion actually is — even, in this story, when he is hiding behind the fountain.
